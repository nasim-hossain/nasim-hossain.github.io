Teaching Experience

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is based on the belief that students learn most effectively when they are motivated by the significance of a topic and can see the purpose of their study. Connecting classroom concepts to real world applications is one of the most effective ways to build that motivation. The Navier-Stokes equations, for example, may not seem particularly interesting on their own, but understanding that they form the foundation of computational fluid dynamics used to model aircraft lift and drag makes the topic far more engaging. This approach applies to almost any subject: showing students how specific knowledge is used in industry, research labs, or technology development helps them recognize why it deserves their attention and effort.

I also believe effective learning should be simplified learning. In mathematically intensive subjects, presenting derivations and proofs alone is often not enough. Students benefit more when they first understand the physical meaning behind the equations, through simple analogies and intuitive examples, before engaging with the rigorous mathematical detail. Active learning reinforces this foundation: short quizzes, question and answer sessions, discussions, and assignments create an environment that continually challenges students to think, building their analytical skills and independence as learners.

These beliefs carry directly into how I mentor. When supervising undergraduate researchers, I make a point of explaining the broader purpose of their work, why a task matters, how it fits into the overall project, and what skills they will carry forward. When students understand the larger picture, they become more motivated, engaged, and committed to learning. My goal as an educator is to combine that motivation with strong foundational knowledge, so that teaching transfers not just information but a lasting curiosity to keep learning.
